# 1.3 Types of Chemical Reactions

The chemical reactions involves the exchange of species in reactants to form products. This exchange of species can take place in many ways leading to different types of chemical reaction. These are of following types

## 1. Combination Reactions

A reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product is called a combination reaction. Examples:

- Reaction of calcium oxide (quick lime) with water to form calcium hydroxide (slaked lime).

Note: Solution of calcium hydroxide (slaked lime produced in above reaction) is used for white washing walls. It reacts slowly with carbon dioxide in air to form a thin layer of calcium carbonate on the walls which gives a shiny appearance to the walls. Calcium carbonate is formed after two to three days of white washing.

$$
\underset{\substack{\text { Calcum } \\ \text { hydroxide }}}{\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_2(a q)}+\mathrm{CO}_2(g) \longrightarrow \underset{\substack{\text { Calcium } \\ \text { carbonate }}}{\mathrm{CaCO}_3(s)}+\mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}(l)
$$

Marble is also represented with the formula $\mathrm{CaCO}_3$.

- Burning of coal:
$$
\underset{\text { Carbon }}{\mathrm{C}(\mathrm{~s})}+\underset{\text { Oxygen }}{\mathrm{O}_2(\mathrm{~g})} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Carbon dioxide }}{\mathrm{CO}_2(\mathrm{~g})}
$$

- Reaction between hydrogen and oxygen gas to form water:
$$
\underset{\text { Hydrogen }}{2 \mathrm{H}_2(\mathrm{~g})}+\underset{\text { Oxygen }}{\mathrm{O}_2(\mathrm{~g})} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Water }}{2 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l})}
$$

## Decomposition Reactions

### Decomposition Details (from text)

- A single reactant breaks into two or more products (opposite of combination).
- Based on energy input:
  - **Thermal decomposition:** uses heat.  
    - $ \text{CaCO}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{CaO} + \text{CO}_2 $ (quick lime; cement manufacturing).  
    - $ \text{FeSO}_4 \cdot 7\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + \text{SO}_2 + \text{SO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} $ (green crystals lose water, then decompose).  
    - $ 2\text{Pb(NO}_3)_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} 2\text{PbO} + 4\text{NO}_2 + \text{O}_2 $ (yellow PbO + brown NO₂ fumes).  
    - Detailed thermal example (lead nitrate):  
$$
\underset{\substack{\text { Lead nitrate } \\ \text { (Colourless) }}}{2 \mathrm{~Pb}\left(\mathrm{NO}_3\right)_2(s)} \xrightarrow{\text { Heat }} \underset{\substack{\text { Lead oxide } \\ \text { (Yellow) }}}{2 \mathrm{PbO}(s)}+\underset{\substack{\text { Nitrogen } \\ \text { dioxide } \\ \text { (Brown fumes) }}}{4 \mathrm{NO}_2(g)}+\underset{\text { Oxygen }}{\mathrm{O}_2(g)}
$$
  - **Electrolysis:** uses electrical energy.  
    - $ 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{Electric current}} 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 $  
    - $ 2\text{NaCl}(l) \xrightarrow{\text{Electric current}} 2\text{Na} + \text{Cl}_2 $

  - **Photolysis (photochemical decomposition):** uses light energy. Examples:  
    - When silver chloride is exposed to sunlight, it decomposes to give silver metal and chlorine gas.  
    - Similarly, silver bromide gives silver metal and bromine gas in the presence of sunlight.  
    - *Note:* Decomposition of silver halides is used in black-and-white photography.
## Exothermic vs Endothermic

## 3. Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Depending upon whether heat is evolved or absorbed during a reaction, the reaction can be exothermic or endothermic.

### (i) Exothermic reactions
The reactions which are accompanied by the release of energy (heat or light) along with the formation of products are called exothermic reactions. Examples:

(a) Respiration: During digestion, carbohydrates (e.g., rice, potatoes) are broken down to glucose. Glucose combines with oxygen in cells to provide energy (special name: respiration).

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{C}_6 \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_6(a q)+6 \mathrm{O}_2(a q) & \longrightarrow 6 \mathrm{CO}_2(a q) +6 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}(l) \\
& + \text { Energy }
\end{aligned}
$$

(b) Burning of natural gas (combustion reaction)

$$
\underset{\text { Methane }}{\mathrm{CH}_4(\mathrm{~g})}+\underset{\text { Oxygen }}{2 \mathrm{O}_2(\mathrm{~g})} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Carbon dioxide }}{\mathrm{CO}_2(\mathrm{~g})}+\underset{\substack{\text { Water } \\ \text { + Heat }}}{2 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~g})}
$$

(c) Burning of magnesium ribbon: burns with dazzling white light.

$$
\underset{\text { Magnesium }}{2 \mathrm{Mg}(s)}+\underset{\text { Oxygen }}{\mathrm{O}_2(g)} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Magnesium oxide }}{2 \mathrm{MgO}(s)}+\text { Heat }
$$

(d) Decomposition of vegetable matter into compost is also exothermic (heat evolved).

### (ii) Endothermic reactions
The reactions which occur by absorption of heat, energy, or light are called endothermic reactions.

(a) Photosynthesis

$$
\begin{aligned}
\underset{\text { Carbon dioxide }}{6 \mathrm{CO}_2(a q)}+12 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}(l) \xrightarrow[\text { Chlorophyll }]{\text { Sunlight }} \mathrm{C}_6 \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_6 & +6 \mathrm{O}_2 \\
& +\underset{\text { Water }}{6 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}}
\end{aligned}
$$

(b) All the decomposition reactions require energy in form of heat, light or electricity for breaking down the reactants.

(c) $\underset{\substack{\text { Barium } \\ \text { hydroxide }}}{\mathrm{Ba}(\mathrm{OH})_2}+\underset{\substack{\text { Ammonium } \\ \text { chloride }}}{2 \mathrm{NH}_4 \mathrm{Cl}} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \underset{\substack{\text { Barium } \\ \text { chloride }}}{\mathrm{BaCl}_2}+\underset{\text { Ammonia }}{2 \mathrm{NH}_3}+2 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{O}$

## Displacement Reactions

- A more reactive element displaces a less reactive one from its compound.  
- Examples:  
  - $ \text{Zn} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{Cu} $  
  - $ \text{Fe} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_4 + \text{Cu} $  
  - $ \text{Pb} + \text{CuCl}_2 \rightarrow \text{PbCl}_2 + \text{Cu} $

## Double Displacement (Precipitation)

- Ions exchange partners; often forms a precipitate.  
- Examples:  
  - $ \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + \text{BaCl}_2 \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_4 \downarrow + 2\text{NaCl} $  
  - $ \text{AgNO}_3 + \text{NaBr} \rightarrow \text{AgBr} \downarrow + \text{NaNO}_3 $
- Neutralisation is also a double displacement: $ \text{NaOH} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{O} $.

## Redox Basics

- Oxidation: addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen.  
- Reduction: addition of hydrogen or removal of oxygen.  
- Redox: both occur together.  
- Examples:  
  - $ \text{CuO} + \text{H}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{Cu} + \text{H}_2\text{O} $ (CuO reduced, H₂ oxidised)  
  - $ \text{ZnO} + \text{C} \rightarrow \text{Zn} + \text{CO} $
- Oxidising agent: causes oxidation, itself reduced (e.g., $ \text{Cl}_2 $, $ \text{MnO}_2 $).  
- Reducing agent: causes reduction, itself oxidised (e.g., C, H₂).
